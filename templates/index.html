Acute Myocardial Infarction (AMI)

Introduction

Cardiovascular diseases (CVDs) remain a leading cause of mortality worldwide, with acute myocardial infarction (AMI), commonly known as a heart attack, posing a significant threat to human health. Early detection and risk assessment of AMI are critical for timely intervention and improved patient outcomes.

About AMI

Definition and Pathophysiology

Acute Myocardial Infarction (AMI), commonly referred to as a heart attack, occurs when blood flow to a part of the heart is obstructed, leading to the death of cardiac muscle cells. The primary cause is the rupture of an atherosclerotic plaque in the coronary artery, triggering thrombus (clot) formation.

Causes and Risk Factors

AMI is predominantly caused by coronary artery disease (CAD), which is the buildup of fatty deposits in the arterial walls. Key risk factors include hypertension, high cholesterol, diabetes, smoking, obesity, and a sedentary lifestyle. Genetic predisposition and advancing age also play significant roles. Identifying these risk factors helps in preventive measures and risk stratification.

Clinical Symptoms

The classic symptoms of AMI include chest pain or discomfort, often described as a squeezing or pressure-like sensation, radiating to the arms, neck, jaw, or back. Other associated symptoms include shortness of breath, sweating, nausea, dizziness, and fatigue. In some cases, especially among women and diabetics, symptoms may be atypical.

Diagnostic Approaches

Diagnosis of AMI involves a combination of clinical evaluation, electrocardiography (ECG), and biomarker analysis. ECG helps detect ST-segment changes indicating myocardial injury. Cardiac biomarkers like troponin and creatine kinase-MB are critical for confirming myocardial cell damage.

Treatment and Management

Prompt treatment is essential to minimize heart muscle damage. Management strategies include reperfusion therapy, such as percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) or thrombolytic therapy, to restore blood flow. Medications like antiplatelets, beta-blockers, statins, and ACE inhibitors are used to stabilize the condition and prevent recurrence.

Prevention and Prognosis

Preventing AMI focuses on controlling modifiable risk factors through lifestyle changes like a healthy diet, regular exercise, and smoking cessation. Routine health screenings for blood pressure, cholesterol, and glucose levels are crucial. With timely intervention and effective management, the prognosis of AMI has significantly improved, although it remains a leading cause of mortality and morbidity globally.
